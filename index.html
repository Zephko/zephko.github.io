Base 11 Space Challenge

Designing and testing PCBs for flight critical electronics in UBC Rocket's rocket launch to space.

The Context

The Base 11 Space Challenge offers a $1M dollar prize to the first student-led team to design, build, and launch a liquid fueled rocket to space. UBC Rocket's Whistler-Blackomb Rocket is attempting to be the winner of this challenge. Our dedicated team meets several times weekly and we are hoping to launch by December 2021.

I am on the Avionics and GNC(Guidance, Navigation and Control) Team. The Whistler-Blackomb space-shot rocket avionics is a redundant system capable of tracking position over time, sequencing the recovery system, detecting apogee, ground and flight termination, fueling, engine control, data acquisition, and ground station communication.

Flight Computer - Data Acquisition and Apogee Detection

I co-designed the Flight Computer PCB which does all the on-board computing on an NXP MK66 32 bit MCU and handles all critical and non-critical tasks such as fueling, apogee detection, recovery deployment, data acquisition, and engine control. This board acquires essential data directly from our GPS, IMU, and our battery management system. This board also uses CAN to receive pressure transducer and thermocouple data from our custom CAN module PCBs located in various parts of our rocket . The board also has an ethernet interface to a Labjack DAQ module which we are hoping will be replaced by our CAN system. We use a combination of buck converters and linear regulators to regulate 24V to 5V and 3V3.

Reaction Control System PCB

I am fully responsible for the whole design process of our Reaction Control System(RCS) PCB including component selection, schematic capture, and layout. The RCS is designed to control the roll of the rocket by activating cold gas thrusters using solenoid valves. This is accomplished by implementing a feedback loop using an NXP microcontroller which reads the angular velocity from an IMU and opens the gas thruster valves using high side switches. Autonomous Sailboat Challenge

Designing a motion planning system to send an autonomous robot to compete in the Vic-Maui International Yacht Race

The Context

UBC Sailbot is an engineering design team that strives to design reliable autonomous sailboats. Since I have been on the team, UBC Sailbot has been focused on building their second large-scale, fully autonomous saiboat, named Raye. The goal was for Raye to compete in the Vic-Maui Yacht Race, a 2300 nautical mile long yacht race that runs from Victoria, BC to Maui, Hawaii. Raye was to be the first fully autonomous vessel to participate in the race. The 2020 Vic-Maui Yacht Race was cancelled due to COVID-19 so we are now aiming for a launch in the summer of 2021.

I have been working on the Motion Planning Software Team. We have been building a system that will chart a course and interface with the controller being developed by our other software subteam.

Motion Planning

Our control system works by having two separate pathfinding modules, an onshore server, and a controller connected using a ROS network. Our global pathfinding module receives GPS and weather data from our onshore server and generates a global path that is sent to our local pathfinding module. Our local pathfinding module connects our global waypoints and avoids nearby obstacles while adapting to changing wind conditions. Path planning for a sailboat has its own unique challenges. At first glance it may seem like you could simply draw a straight line from Victoria to Maui. However, there are some major challenges that our system takes into account:

1. Collision Avoidance

There are hundreds of boats moving around the Pacific Ocean at any given time. Our pathfinding needs to find a safe path by taking into account the location, heading, and speed of all these boats.

2. Wind Conditions

Our sailboat relies on sufficient wind speeds to allow us to move. On a global pathfinding scale our path must stay in areas with optimal wind conditions. With constantly changing wind Conditions this can be difficult.

On a local pathfinding scale, our boat must always avoid sailing upwind or downwind. To move towards a destination that lies upwind or downwind, we try to always move at 30 degrees from the upwind direction and use tacking(zig-zag) maneuvers. To do this we set the cost of travelling in an upwind or downwind direction to be very high.

3. Minimize Path Length

Our local pathfinding system uses the RRT* (Rapidly-exploring random tree) algorithm. This algorithm works by growing a tree from the start point. Every iteration the algorithm chooses a random point in the search space, checks its validity, and attempts to connect it to the nearest point on the tree.

Since ideally our sailboat has to adjust its path the minimum amount of times, much of my contribution was focused on making validity checking functions that take into account the predicted location of boats in the future by extrapolating them in our state space based on the boats' heading and speed.

Local Path Visualizer

One of my main contributions to the team was working on the local path visulizer which is built using matplotlib. The visualizer subscribes to our different topics in the ROS network and displays the current global/local waypoints, wind direction, nearby obstacles, and the current path. The visualizer constantly updates and is capable of being sped up so we can quickly test pathfinding scenarios.
